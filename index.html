Session Brief

Changing Gender Norms — Who Changes, How, and Why It's Complicated

Purpose of This Session

Gender norms — the informal rules that shape how people are expected to behave as men and women — are shifting in societies around the world. But this change is neither uniform nor straightforward. Some norms are eroding quickly; others are proving deeply resistant. Some groups are driving change; others are pushing back. And in some cases, the people most affected by norms are the last to benefit when they change.

This session asks you to examine this complexity directly. Working in a small group, you will investigate how gender norms are changing in a specific domain, present your analysis to the full cohort, and engage critically with the analyses of other groups.

Session Structure

The Analytical Framework

Regardless of your domain, your group's analysis should address these three questions drawn from ALIGN's flagship report Gender, Power and Progress:

Question 1
How fast is change happening — and is it keeping pace with changing attitudes?

Norm change in behavior often lags behind shifts in attitudes. Where is the gap largest in your domain, and why?

Question 2
Who is being left behind?

Norm change rarely benefits everyone equally. Which groups — defined by geography, class, age, race, or other factors — are experiencing the least change, or are being actively harmed by norm shifts?

Question 3
Where is resistance concentrating, and why?

Some norms change with little friction; others face organized, sustained resistance. What explains the pattern of resistance in your domain?

Presentation Guidelines

Your group's presentation should be 8–10 minutes, followed by a 5-minute response from another group. It should cover:

  • A brief framing of your domain: what gender norms are at stake, and what the baseline looked like
  • Evidence of how those norms are changing — drawing on at least two of the resources above
  • Analysis through the three cross-cutting questions (pace of change, who is left behind, where resistance concentrates)
  • One open question your group was unable to resolve — something you would want to investigate further

Your group should prepare a slide deck to accompany the presentation. Slides should be clear and concise — use them to display key evidence and data points, rather than as a script. Aim for no more than 8–10 slides.

Group 1

Education

How are gender norms in schools and educational systems changing — and what are the consequences for both girls and boys?

Education is one of the most powerful drivers of gender norm change, but it can also reinforce the norms it is meant to challenge. Girls have made dramatic gains in school access and attainment over the past 25 years — but a "hidden curriculum" in many schools still transmits traditional gender expectations. At the same time, a growing body of evidence points to boys falling behind educationally in many contexts, raising new questions about how changing norms affect men and boys differently.

Questions to guide your analysis

  • Where have gender norms in education shifted most, and where have they proved most resistant?
  • How do "manfluencers" and online culture interact with what students are learning in school?
  • What does the evidence say about what makes gender-transformative education actually work?
  • How do crises (economic shocks, conflict, pandemics) tend to affect gender norms in education?

Group 2

Paid and Unpaid Work and Care

How are norms around who works, who earns, and who cares for families shifting — and why is behavior lagging so far behind attitudes?

This domain surfaces one of the most striking paradoxes in gender norm research: attitudes have changed dramatically over the past quarter century, but actual behavior — particularly around unpaid care and domestic work — has changed very little. Women are 55% more likely than men to take career breaks, and nearly three times as likely to be responsible for childcare. Understanding why the attitude-behavior gap is so persistent is central to this group's analysis.

Questions to guide your analysis

  • Where is the gap between stated attitudes and actual behavior largest — and what explains it?
  • How does the care economy intersect with women's economic participation? What would it take to revalue care work?
  • How are men's roles as caregivers changing — or not — across different national contexts?
  • What policy approaches (parental leave, childcare provision, equal pay legislation) have shown evidence of shifting norms, not just outcomes?

Most relevant resources

  • ALIGN, Gender, Power and Progress — Paid and Unpaid Work and Care section: alignplatform.org/gender-power-progress
  • WEF, Global Gender Gap Report 2025 — Economic Participation and Opportunity dimension: weforum.org
  • Equimundo, State of the World's Fathers: equimundo.org
  • Ipsos, Mind the Gaps (2026) — household responsibility findings: ipsos.com
  • UN Women, Social Norms, Gender and Development (2023): unwomen.org/en/digital-library/publications/2023/10/discussion-paper-social-norms-gender-and-development
Group 3

Political Voice and Representation

How are norms around women's and men's participation in political and civic life evolving — and what is actually driving change where it happens?

Women's parliamentary representation has doubled globally since 1997. The share of people agreeing that "men make better political leaders" has fallen from 50% to 35%. Yet political empowerment remains the largest remaining gender gap in the WEF index — 162 years from parity at the current pace. And resistance is intensifying: the 2024 super-election year saw far-right gains in multiple countries, with gender weaponized as a political tool. This group examines both the progress and the backlash.

Questions to guide your analysis

  • What has actually driven gains in women's political representation where they have occurred? (Quotas? Social movements? Role models? Economic development?)
  • How does the online environment — harassment, disinformation, coordinated attacks — function as a barrier to women's political participation?
  • How are men engaging (or not) in championing gender-equitable political agendas?
  • What does the 2024 electoral landscape tell us about the relationship between norm change and political backlash?

Most relevant resources

  • ALIGN, Gender, Power and Progress — Political Voice and Representation section: alignplatform.org/gender-power-progress
  • WEF, Global Gender Gap Report 2025 — Political Empowerment dimension: weforum.org
  • Ipsos, Mind the Gaps (2026): ipsos.com
  • Pew Research Center, Views of the Impact of Changing Gender Roles (2024): pewresearch.org
  • Carnegie Endowment, The New Global Struggle Over Gender, Rights, and Family Values (2025): carnegieendowment.org
Group 4

Health and Well-being

How are gender norms shaping health outcomes, health-seeking behavior, and well-being for men and women — and where is norm change happening, and where is it stalling?

Gender norms shape health in profound and often underexamined ways. Norms around femininity influence how women's health concerns are taken seriously by healthcare systems. Norms around masculinity discourage men from seeking help, with significant consequences for mental health and longevity. Fertility expectations shape women's life choices across very different cultural contexts. And the burden of caregiving — itself a gendered norm — has direct consequences for the health and well-being of caregivers, who are disproportionately women.

Questions to guide your analysis

  • How do gender norms shape men's and women's health-seeking behavior differently — and what are the consequences?
  • Where have norms around fertility and family size changed most, and what has driven that change?
  • How does the gendered burden of unpaid caregiving affect the health and well-being of caregivers?
  • What norms are proving most resistant to change in this domain, and what explains that resistance?

Most relevant resources

  • ALIGN, Gender, Power and Progress — Sexual and Reproductive Health section: alignplatform.org/gender-power-progress
  • WEF, Global Gender Gap Report 2025 — Health and Survival dimension: weforum.org
  • UNDP, Gender Social Norms Index 2023: hdr.undp.org
  • Equimundo, IMAGES Global Report — men's health-seeking behavior data: equimundo.org
  • UN Women, Social Norms, Gender and Development (2023): unwomen.org/en/digital-library/publications/2023/10/discussion-paper-social-norms-gender-and-development
Group 5

Masculinities and Social Change

How are norms about what it means to be a man changing — and what evidence do we have that they are hardening in some populations even as they loosen in others?

This is arguably the most contested domain in contemporary gender norm research. On one hand, definitions of manhood are broadening in many contexts, with more men embracing caregiving roles and rejecting rigid emotional scripts. On the other hand, the Equimundo IMAGES data show that adherence to the "Man Box" — rigid norms of toughness, self-sufficiency, and dominance — is increasing among young men globally. The Stanford/SIEPR research adds a further dimension: men are often more supportive of gender equality than they think their peers are, suggesting that misperception itself is a driver of norm maintenance.

Questions to guide your analysis

  • What does the evidence show about how masculinity norms are changing — and for which men, in which contexts?
  • What are the consequences of rigid masculinity norms for men themselves (mental health, loneliness, help-seeking)?
  • How does online culture — the manosphere, social media influencers — function as a mechanism for transmitting and hardening masculinity norms among younger generations?
  • What does it look like when masculinity norms change positively? What drives it?

Most relevant resources

  • Equimundo, IMAGES Global Report: equimundo.org/images-research
  • Equimundo, State of the World's Fathers: equimundo.org/state-of-the-worlds-fathers-research
  • Stanford/SIEPR, "Surprising Insights from a Global Study on Perceptions of Gender Norms" (2023): siepr.stanford.edu
  • Pew Research Center, How Americans See Men and Masculinity (2024): pewresearch.org
  • Ipsos, Mind the Gaps (2026) — Gen Z men findings: ipsos.com

Resource Guide

Annotated descriptions of all resources available for the session.

Section 1 — Conceptual Foundations

These resources explain what gender norms are, how they work, and how they change. If your group needs to establish a shared analytical vocabulary before diving into evidence, start here.

ALIGN Platform — Conceptual Resources on Gender Norms

"About Norms"

alignplatform.org/about-norms  |  Conceptual explainer

A concise explainer covering what gender norms are and how they differ from social norms. Particularly useful for understanding the two main disciplinary approaches: the sociological/feminist view (norms are embedded in institutions; changing them requires structural change) and the social psychology view (norms are beliefs about what others expect; changing them requires correcting misperceptions).

Gender, Power and Progress: How Norms Change

alignplatform.org/gender-power-progress  |  Flagship report | Available in English, French, and Spanish

ALIGN's flagship report reviewing 25 years of gender norm change since the UN's 1995 Beijing Platform for Action across four sectors: education, sexual and reproductive health, paid and unpaid work and care, and political voice and representation. Three cross-cutting observations serve as the analytical framework for this session: (1) norm change is slow and non-linear; (2) change is uneven and the most disadvantaged are often left furthest behind; (3) progress repeatedly stalls at the point where women are poised to achieve significant change. The summary version (~20 pages) is sufficient.

Section 2 — Empirical Data

These resources provide quantitative evidence on gender gaps and norm change across countries and populations.

WEF — Global Gender Gap Report 2025

weforum.org/publications/global-gender-gap-report-2025  |  Annual report | 148 economies

The most comprehensive cross-national dataset on gender gaps, published annually since 2006. Measures four dimensions: Health and Survival (96.2% closed), Educational Attainment (95.1% closed), Economic Participation and Opportunity (61.0% closed), and Political Empowerment (22.9% closed). At the current pace, full parity is 123 years away.

Key findings: women now outperform men at tertiary education but hold only 28.8% of top management roles; women are 55.2% more likely than men to take career breaks; Latin America and the Caribbean is the fastest-moving region. The report's regional data and country rankings are particularly useful for comparative analysis.

UNDP — Gender Social Norms Index 2023 (GSNI)

hdr.undp.org/system/files/documents/hdp-document/gsni202303.pdf  |  Report | 80+ countries

Unlike most gender indices, the GSNI measures attitudes rather than outcomes — specifically, how many people hold biased views against gender equality. Its most striking finding: even as gender outcome gaps have narrowed, the percentage of people holding biased gender norms has barely changed, and has increased in some regions. Changing what people can do is not the same as changing what people believe is appropriate.

Ipsos — Mind the Gaps: Global Attitudes Toward Gender Equality in 2026

ipsos.com/en/international-womens-day-2026  |  Survey report | 29 countries, ~23,000 respondents

Ipsos's annual International Women's Day survey. The 2026 edition finds that a slim majority (52%) across 29 countries now say gender equality efforts have "gone far enough." Key findings: 54% of men feel they are doing "too much" for equality; 31% of Gen Z men agree a wife should always obey her husband — twice the rate of Baby Boomer men; young women are significantly more optimistic about their futures than young men are about theirs.

The generational reversal — where the youngest cohort shows the sharpest gender divide in attitudes — is one of the most discussion-worthy findings. Also see the 2025 edition.

Pew Research Center — Changing Gender Roles and Masculinity (2024)

How Americans See Men and Masculinity

pewresearch.org/social-trends/2024/10/17/how-americans-see-men-and-masculinity  |  Survey report | U.S. sample

Views of the Impact of Changing Gender Roles

pewresearch.org/social-trends/2024/10/17/views-of-the-impact-of-changing-gender-roles  |  Survey report | U.S. sample

Two companion surveys on shifting gender norms. Key findings: only 42% of men describe themselves as "highly masculine," with sharp variation by political affiliation (53% of Republican men vs. 29% of Democratic men). Most Americans (81%) say women's gains have not come at men's expense, but men are twice as likely as women to disagree. U.S.-focused, but patterns connect clearly to global data from Ipsos and Equimundo. Most relevant to Groups 3 and 5.

Equimundo

International Men and Gender Equality Survey (IMAGES)

equimundo.org/images-research  |  Longitudinal survey | Multi-country

The most rigorous global evidence base on men's attitudes and practices around gender norms — 15 years of longitudinal data published jointly with UNFPA. Key finding: adherence to rigid "Man Box" norms (extreme self-sufficiency, toughness, dominance) is increasing among young men globally, not decreasing. More than 60% of men report feeling that no one cares whether they are okay. Essential for Group 5; relevant to Groups 1 and 4.

State of the World's Fathers

equimundo.org/state-of-the-worlds-fathers-research  |  Biennial report | Multi-country

Tracks norm change around men's engagement in caregiving and domestic work. Consistently finds that men want to be more involved in care than social and workplace norms allow — the gap between stated preferences and actual behavior is itself evidence of norm constraint. Most relevant to Groups 2 and 5.

Section 3 — Research Reviews and Novel Findings

These resources offer synthesis across bodies of evidence, or introduce specific findings not captured by the larger datasets above.

UN Women — Social Norms, Gender and Development: A Review of Research and Practice (2023)

unwomen.org/en/digital-library/publications/2023/10/discussion-paper-social-norms-gender-and-development  |  Discussion paper

A systematic review of evidence on how social norms shape gender outcomes across development contexts, and what interventions have shown evidence of actually shifting them. More recent than the ALIGN flagship report (2023 vs. 2020) and development-focused. Useful for groups examining lower-income country contexts.

Stanford/SIEPR — "Surprising Insights from a Global Study on Perceptions of Gender Norms" (2023)

siepr.stanford.edu/news/surprising-insights-global-study-perceptions-gender-norms  |  Research summary | 60-country Gallup study

A study by Alessandra Voena and collaborators finds that people worldwide systematically misperceive others as more conservative on gender than they actually are. In Zimbabwe, men estimated only 37% of male peers support women working outside the home; the actual figure was 72. The implication: norm change may be achievable faster than expected simply by correcting these misperceptions. Relevant to all groups, but especially Group 5.
